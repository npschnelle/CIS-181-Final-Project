History

The name Lille is derived from the Old French name for the city, L'Île, because of its original settlement being between arms of the Deûle River, an important trading route for the region. Baudoin V, Count of Flanders, is the first to mention the city in a 1066 charter. Throughout the Middle Ages and the Renaissance, the city was under many different rulers due to its flourishing trade. It was only in 1667 that Lille became part of France, though it was briefly occupied by the Dutch during the Spanish War of Succession.

Though Lille has always been a site of trade, the city, along with suburbs Roubaix and Tourcoing, the city became an industrial hub in the 19th century, specializing in the manufacturing of chemicals, metals, and most importantly, textiles. Lille was also important for food processing due to its connection with famous microbiologist Louis Pasteur. It was during this time that Lille expanded, annexing neighboring communities to triple its square mileage and double its population.

Lille has long been fought over by European powers, and the 20th century was no different. The city was occupied by the Germans during both world wars, and because of this damage in combination with the industrial crisis of the 1980s, Lille's economy was devastated. Since the 1990s, Lille has been filling the space left behind by manufacturing with financial, administrative, and research facilities, as well as universities.

Famous Inhabitants

Charles de Gaulle

Charles de Gaulle was born in Lille in November of 1890. He first joined the French military in 1913 after graduating from a military academy near Versailles. He fought in World War 1's longest battle, the Battle of Verdun, and continued to serve in various positions and locations for the French military between the end of World War 1 and the start of World War 2. During that time, he began his writing and political careers.

After being dismissed and sentenced to death for encouraging the war to continue against the Pétain government's goal of an armistice, de Gaulle became the leader of what would become the Free French Forces. After the end of World War 2, de Gaulle led the provisional governments of France before resigning in 1946. He would go on to found a political party before becoming president in 1958, a position he would serve for 10 years.

Louis Pasteur

Louis Pasteur was born in December of 1822 in eastern France. After earning his master's of science in 1845, Pasteur worked as a chemist and professor at the University of Strasbourg, where he studied molecular asymmetry. His work in this field led to the creation of a new field of study called spatial chemistry.

In 1854, Pasteur was appointed Dean of the Faculty of Sciences in Lille, where he began his work in biology. After being asked by local distilleries to investigate the cause of variation in alcohol production, Louis Pasteur began studying fermentation and discovered that living organisms were responsible for it. This research was foundational in the field of microbiology and would eventually lead to the development of his eponymous food-preservation system and the world’s first rabies vaccine.
